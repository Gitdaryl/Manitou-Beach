SECTION 6: FISHING GUIDE
========================================

The fishing guide at /fishing is a comprehensive resource for fishing Devils Lake and Round Lake. It covers species, tackle, best times, and seasonal tips.

SPECIES IN DEVILS LAKE AND ROUND LAKE:

1. Largemouth Bass — The king of Devils Lake. Ambush predators near docks, fallen timber, and weed edges. Aggressive fighters. Best bait: plastic worms (Texas rig), jigs with crawfish trailer, topwater frogs, spinnerbaits. Tackle: medium-heavy rod, 12-17 lb fluorocarbon or braid. Best time: dawn and dusk, especially the first two hours after sunrise. Peak season: June-July spawn through October. Healthy, naturally reproducing population.

2. Smallmouth Bass — Pound-for-pound one of the hardest-fighting freshwater fish. Found along rocky shoreline areas and gravelly points. Best bait: tube jigs, drop shot with finesse worm, small crankbaits, live crayfish. Tackle: medium rod, 8-12 lb fluorocarbon. Best season: spring pre-spawn and fall. More common near rocky areas on the north shore.

3. Bluegill — The ultimate family fish and best eating in the lake. Excellent numbers. Beds visible from shore in 2-4 feet of water during the June spawn. Great for kids and beginners. Best bait: small worms on #8 hook, crickets, small jigs (1/32 oz), wax worms. Tackle: light rod, 4-6 lb monofilament, small bobber. Averaged 7 inches in DNR surveys — 70% legal size. One of the healthiest panfish populations in the region.

4. Northern Pike — The Tip-Up Festival star. Aggressive with a mouth full of teeth — use a wire leader. Through the ice in February they're at their most accessible. Best bait: large swimbaits, live suckers or shiners for ice fishing, tip-ups with sucker minnow. Tackle: medium-heavy to heavy rod, 20-30 lb braid, steel or titanium wire leader. A 36-inch-plus fish is a real trophy on Devils Lake.

5. Black Crappie — Light flaky meat that fries up beautifully. School up in spring near submerged structure like brush piles, dock pilings, and fallen trees. Best bait: small tube jigs (1/16-1/8 oz), crappie jigs with marabou, small minnows under bobber. Tackle: ultralight rod, 4-6 lb monofilament. Best spring action near dock structures in 4-8 feet of water.

6. Yellow Perch — Michigan's favorite panfish. Where you find one, you find a hundred. Sweet, firm white meat. A Devils Lake winter staple through the ice. Best bait: small jigs tipped with wax worm, live minnows, perch rigs. Tackle: ultralight to light rod, 4-6 lb monofilament. Averaged 9 inches-plus in DNR surveys — above state average.

7. Pumpkinseed Sunfish — One of the most colorful freshwater fish in Michigan. Orange and blue markings that look almost tropical. Abundant near weed beds. A favorite for kids and great on ultralight tackle. Best bait: small worms, wax worms, tiny jigs.

8. Brown Bullhead — Michigan's classic catfish. Night fishing for bullheads on warm summer evenings is a Manitou Beach tradition — cast and wait. Best bait: night crawlers, chicken liver, stink bait. Tackle: medium rod, 8-10 lb monofilament, bottom rig. Best after dark, May through August.

FISHING SEASONS:

Spring — Bass spawn in the shallows late May through June. Pike are aggressive post ice-out. Best topwater action of the year. Tip: target weed edges at dawn. Bluegill nests visible in 2-4 ft of water.

Summer — Largemouth bass hold on deep structure during midday heat. Early morning and evening topwater is productive on both lakes. Round Lake walleye troll at 10-15 ft. Tip: 7 AM and 7 PM are prime windows. Dock fishing produces bass all day on Devils Lake.

Fall — Feeding frenzy before winter. Bass, pike, and walleye all pack on weight. Some of the best fishing of the year. Round Lake perch can be exceptional in October. Tip: jerkbaits and spinnerbaits through October.

Ice Fishing — February means Tip-Up Festival. Northern pike, walleye, bluegill, crappie, and perch through the ice on both lakes. Tip: 6-8 inches of clear ice typically by mid-January. Always check DNR ice conditions before venturing out.

Round Lake is the better lake for walleye. Devils Lake is the better lake for bass and the ice fishing tradition.

SECTION 8: THE LAKES
========================================

DEVILS LAKE
Page: /devils-lake
1,330 acres with 600+ boat slips. Locals call it "the party lake." It's the center of community life in Manitou Beach — boating, fishing, festivals, fireworks, and generations of family memories. The community has been coming back every summer since the 1870s.

The public boat launch is on Devils Lake Highway with a free paved ramp and 60-car trailer parking. The Manitou Beach Marina at 9517 Devils Lake Hwy has 600+ slips for seasonal dockage.

ROUND LAKE
Page: /round-lake
515 acres, 67 feet deep — the quieter side of lake life. Connected to Devils Lake by a shallow channel at Cherry Point. A glacial kettle lake carved around 10,000 BC. No bars or marinas — just homes, families, and serious fishing. Same zip code (49253) and township (Rollin) as Devils Lake. DNR access site for kayaks, canoes, and small boats.

Round Lake is known for walleye fishing. It's the calmer, more peaceful lake compared to the social scene on Devils Lake.

SECTION 13: HISTORY OF MANITOU BEACH
========================================

Pre-1830: Potawatomi and Ojibwa people camped along the shores of Devils Lake.

1870s: Grand hotels, a dance pavilion, bathhouses, and two railroad stations made Devils Lake a top Michigan summer destination. People came by train from Detroit and Toledo.

1888: Manitou Beach was officially platted as a community.

1920s-1940s: The Yacht Club was established. The community solidified as a summer destination.

1950s: The Tip-Up Festival launched, becoming one of Michigan's most beloved winter traditions.

Today: Boating, fishing, festivals, and a tight-knit lake community that keeps coming back generation after generation.

PRONUNCIATION NOTE: "Manitou" is pronounced "MAN-ih-taw" (rhymes with "saw"), not "MAN-ih-too." Locals are protective of this.

SECTION 17: EVENT ORGANIZER GUIDE
========================================

This is the complete walkthrough for anyone who wants to put on an event in the Manitou Beach area — from a free community barbecue to a ticketed festival with vendor booths.

SUBMITTING A FREE EVENT LISTING:

First, head to /submit-event. You'll see a form that collects everything we need to get your event on the community calendar. Here's what you'll fill in:

Start with your event name — make it catchy, this is what people see first on the calendar. Then pick your event date. If it spans multiple days, there's an end date field too. Set your start and end times so people know when to show up and when it wraps.

For location, type in the venue name and address — people need to know where to go. Write a description that tells the story of your event: what it is, why someone should come, what to expect. If there's a door price, you can add that in the cost field. If it's free, just leave it blank.

Next, pick your event type. You've got six options:

"Just show up" — no registration needed, perfect for open community events.
